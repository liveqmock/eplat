•Is the patient stable or unstable?
•How do you define “unstable”?
C“Hemodynamically unstable” is defined by the following signs and symptoms: chest pain, shortness of breath, decreased level of consciousness, low blood pressure, shock, pulmonary congestion, congestive heart failure, and AMI.
•
•Differentiate sinus tachycardia from supraventricular tachycardia, highlighting the fact that sinus tachycardia is usually a normal compensatory mechanism. Therefore, if a patient with sinus tachycardia is unstable, the cause is usually not a rate problem but a problem with volume or pumping.
•Atrial flutter and atrial fibrillation may cause hemodynamic compromise in some patients. New-onset atrial fibrillation frequently produces ventricular rates higher than 160 bpm. If atrial flutter (2:1 block) occurs with no hemodynamic compromise, synchronized electrical cardioversion may be performed electively instead of on an emergency basis. Differentiate elective cardioversion from emergent cardioversion.
•Watch out! In patients with chronic atrial fibrillation, a thrombus may develop in the atrium and could lead to a cerebral thromboembolic stroke.
•PSVT in the adult may produce ventricular rates as high as 240 to 250 bpm. If a stable PSVT deteriorates to an unstable PSVT during treatment, progress to synchronized cardioversion.
•Stress that approximately 80% of wide-complex tachyarrhythmias are VT. Also discuss typical postcardioversion medication therapy of VT, including lidocaine, procainamide, and bretylium. Review the appropriate doses and IV infusion rates.
• Discuss antiarrhythmic therapies for atrial fibrillation/flutter.
• Anticoagulants should be considered for the patient who does not respond to attempted cardioversion for atrial fibrillation.
•Describe synchronization mode on the defibrillator. Allow participants to examine the equipment and locate synchronization mode. Show participants how to synchronize when the monitor is not sensing the QRS complex.

•Stress that approximately 80% of wide-complex tachyarrhythmias are VT. Also discuss typical postcardioversion medication therapy of VT, including lidocaine, procainamide, and bretylium. Review the appropriate doses and IV infusion rates.
•Explain that the treatment for unstable narrow- and wide-complex tachyarrhythmias is synchronized cardioversion. Differentiation of aberration and ectopy in a rapidly deteriorating patient is esoteric.
•Review the procedure for synchronized cardioversion. Demonstrate the procedure on equipment that is comparable to equipment used by the participants. Many EMS systems have “hands-free” defibrillation.
•Solicit participant responses.
•Complications may include deterioration to VF, embolization of a thrombus, etc. •If the rhythm deteriorates to VF, instruct the participants to rapidly assess cardiovascular status and proceed to immediate defibrillation.
•
•Sedatives may be used for the awake, hemodynamically unstable patient. Some facilities prefer to use anesthesia for elective synchronized cardioversion attempts. The hemodynamic status of the patient and the potential for further deterioration should be considered. Watch for apnea or hypoventilation after sedation. Frequent monitoring of vital signs after cardioversion is essential until stabilization occurs.
