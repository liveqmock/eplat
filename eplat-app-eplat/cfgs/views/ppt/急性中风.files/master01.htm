•Answer 1: You conclude that the patient has had a stroke or transient ischemic attack (TIA).  Stroke = sudden focal neurologic deficits or loss of consciousness caused by acute disruption in brain blood supply. TIA = a reversible episode of focal dysfunction secondary to transient occlusion of an artery. A TIA can be thought of as “cerebral angina” and is an important forecaster of stroke (permanent cell damage). Clinical signs persist, making stroke more likely than TIA. Right-sided findings are consistent with a left-sided stroke.

•Answer 2: The NIHSS measures neurologic function, which correlates with ischemic stroke severity and long-term outcome. It can be performed in 7 minutes and can be performed simultaneously with initial admission procedures, so it should not delay other assessment or therapy. It enables performance of serial standardized neurologic evaluation over time by a nurse or physician. This stroke scale is shown in the ACLS Textbook and the 2000 ECC Handbook.

•Answer 1: Management of blood pressure in stroke patients is controversial. Few data exist even to provide consensus recommendations. The guidelines provided in the ACLS Textbook are tailored to stroke type (ischemic versus hemorrhagic) and vary if thrombolytic therapy is provided. In general, emergent antihypertensive therapy is unnecessary for most stroke patients. However, aggressive control of blood pressure is provided if hemorrhagic stroke is present or thrombolytic therapy has been administered.

•Answer 1: In the first hours after an ischemic stroke the CT scan is usually normal. After 3 to 12 hours the ischemic or infarcted area of the stroke swells with edema and evolves into an area of hypodensity. A well-defined hypodensity indicating blocked blood flow is rarely observed within the first 3 hours of a stroke. Patients with well-defined hypodensities should not be treated with fibrinolytics.
•Answer 2: If course participants are unfamiliar with evaluating CT scans, instructors should point out that hypodense areas indicate lack of blood flow as well as excessive fluid (CSF) and edema; hyperdense areas indicate blood, which is slightly more dense than brain tissue. Acute subarachnoid bleeding spreads rapidly throughout the subarachnoid space, producing hyperdense (white) areas on the CT scan throughout much of the subarachnoid space.

•The large majority of CT scans obtained within 3 hours of an acute ischemic stroke will be normal. Three hours is not enough time for swelling and edema to occur. A massive stroke, occluding the blood supply of an entire hemisphere, however, can often be seen on CT scans. The underperfused brain appears hypodense and darker than the unaffected side. This finding is a contraindication to fibrinolytic therapy.

•Treatment should include
•Close monitoring and control of blood pressure
•Continuous ECG monitoring because arrhythmias can occur after stroke
•Assessment and support of cardiorespiratory function, including use of pulse oximetry and oxygen if indicated
•Evaluation and treatment of blood glucose if needed
•Administration of thiamine (100 mg) if the patient is malnourished or alcoholic
•Control of fever with acetaminophen
•Do not administer aspirin or heparin for 24 hours after administration of fibrinolytics (although previous aspirin administration is not a contraindication to fibrinolytics).
•Keep patient NPO.
•Monitor neurologic status closely.
