Transcutaneous Pacing for Asystole
•TCP is a short-term intervention performed through large pacing electrodes positioned on the patient’s chest and back. TCP stimulates the heart through the skin and muscles of the chest wall; the heart contracts and maintains cardiac output.
•Continue CPR chest compressions during pacing. Stop compressions if the paced complexes produce a measurable pulse.
•Proceed with administration of medications (epinephrine and atropine) independent of TCP.
•Turn off the pacer periodically to eliminate pacing artifact and examine the ECG.
•Look for return of promising ECG features such as P waves or QRS complexes.
Epinephrine in Asystole
Every cardiac arrest victim should receive epinephrine except victims who respond to
interventions before rescuers achieve IV access and VF patients who receive
vasopressin. Epinephrine increases diastolic blood pressure, blood flow to the brain,
and blood flow to the heart, specifically the coronary arteries.
Atropine in Asystole
•Atropine is recommended based on an unconfirmed assumption: excessive vagal or parasympathetic tone can stop ventricular and supraventricular pacemaker activity.
•Avoid atropine in cases where the lack of cardiac activity has a clear explanation, such as in hypothermic arrest.
•Atropine can vary by dose (0.03 to 0.04 mg/kg) and interval (every 3 to 5 minutes).
