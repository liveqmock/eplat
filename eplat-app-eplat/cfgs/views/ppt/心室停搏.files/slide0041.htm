Termination of Resuscitation Attempts
“Do Not Start” criteria for not starting resuscitative efforts: valid DNAR order, clear expression of a patient's self-determination, or obvious signs of death. When none is apparent, resuscitative efforts should start rapidly, proceed aggressively and effectively, and terminate sensitively.
Asystole as Confirmation of Death
Asystole is seen most often as the terminal rhythm in a resuscitative effort that started with a different rhythm but as the first rhythm when responding to an emergency.
Most often asystole is a confirmation of death rather than a “rhythm” or a patient who can be resuscitated if the attempt persists long enough. Persistent asystole represents extensive myocardial ischemia and damage from prolonged periods of inadequate coronary perfusion. Such a status has a grim prognosis.
Criteria for Stopping Resuscitative Efforts
Know the criteria for stopping resuscitative efforts set by your prehospital care system or wherever you provide professional services. Stop resuscitative efforts if there is no response after successful initiation of standard interventions, provided this is not at variance with local policies and cessation is approved by an authorized physician.
Stop Efforts in the Field: NO Transport if Patients Need Continued CPR
If these criteria (no response to full ACLS interventions) are met in an out-of-hospital setting, resuscitative efforts should stop, either under existing physician-authorized protocols or after direct voice contact with the online medical control physician. Extensive evidence confirms that with rare exception (eg, hypothermia, electrocution) there is no physiological value to transporting patients in asystolic cardiac arrest to an ED or hospital.
