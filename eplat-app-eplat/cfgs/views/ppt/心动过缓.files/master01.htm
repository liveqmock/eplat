•Which is most likely her rhythm?
•She experienced no chest pain or ischemic symptoms prior to the onset of her weakness and shortness of breath. Therefore, the only rhythm not associated with acute pain, rhythm A, is the more likely cause of her symptomatic bradycardia. Rhythm A is due to increased para- sympathetic tone or medication side effects. Rhythm B is unlikely because it is often seen after acute ischemia, though it can occur from toxic drug effects. Rhythm C is almost always caused by an occlusion of the left anterior descending artery. AMIs associated with that artery are large, serious, and painful.

•Some Examples to Consider Using
•Clinical Information: a man is having an acute myocardial infarction. He has severe chest pain, HR = 40; BP = 80/55.
•Comment: It is important to decide the sequence of chest pain, bradycardia, and hypotension. Chest pain before bradycardia suggests that the AMI is causing the bradycardia and hypotension. 
•If the person gets dizzy, lightheaded, and weak, and then gets the chest pain, the sequence may be vaso-vagal event = bradycardia = low cardiac perfusion = drop in pressure = ischemic myocardium = ischemic pain begins.

•Determining the Rate
•1. Find where the R-S junction of a QRS complex falls on one of the darker 5-mm lines.
•2. Note the next R-S junction; mark it.
•3. Label each of the darker 5-mm lines between the first R-S junction and the next one in the following sequence:
• 300 - 150 - 100 - 75 - 60 - 50
•4. This number is the rate of beats per minute.

•Sinus bradycardia
•Rate = 48 bpm; regular.
•The pathology is a decrease in the rate of atrial depolarization secondary to sinus node disease, increased parasympathetic tone, or the effects of drugs such as digoxin, b-blockers, or calcium channel blockers.
•
•Second-degree type I AV block
•Progressive lengthening of the P-R interval until a P wave is NOT followed by a QRS complex.
•Atrial rhythm is regular.
•Ventricular rhythm has pauses because every 4th P wave fails to conduct into the ventricles. (4 P waves to 3 QRS complexes = a 4:3 cycle.)
•Note: Progressive prolongation of the P-R interval. This means
•increasing conduction delay in AV node before the non-conducted beat.

•Second-degree type II AV block
•3 conducted beats are followed by 2 non-conducted P waves.
•The P-R interval of conducted beats remains constant.
•Block is usually located at the level of the bundle branches.
•QRS is wide because of the block location near the bundle branches.
•Cause: a serious organic lesion in the conduction pathway.
•Prognosis: usually poor.

•SUGGESTIONS TO INSTRUCTORS
•Keep repeating how simple the treatment of symptomatic bradycardias is.
•Treatment summary
•1. If there are serious signs or symptoms due to the bradycardia: treat the bradycardia.
•2. Treat using this intervention sequence:
atropine
 transcutaneous pacing
 dopamine
 epinephrine
 isoproterenol
•3. If there are NOT serious signs and symptoms, ask whether the bradycardia fits the criteria for one of the “red-flag” bradycardias:
 Type II second-degree AV block or third-degree AV block.
•4. If a “red-flag” bradycardia is present: prepare for transvenous pacing while using transcutaneous pacing.
•Atropine is for temporary stabilization only.
•Dopamine infusions are useful for patients who do not respond to atropine and those for whom TCP is delayed or impossible due to non-capture, unavailability, or malfunction.

•Some Pearls and Perils to share about treating symptomatic bradycardia:
•Why does capture sometimes fail?
•A large percentage of awake patients experience enough discomfort to require medications.
•Give small doses of a narcotic and/or benzodiazepines.
•Clinically assess the patient continuously: capture will cease if the ventricles fibrillate.
•A pacing shock on a T wave may induce VF, but this is extremely rare.
•PVCs are frequently not sensed by pacemakers.
•Catecholamine infusions
•Dopamine infusion
CUse if unresponsive to atropine and TCP is unavailable or ineffective
CTransvenous pacing as soon as possible
CSame doses as for cardiogenic shock
•Epinephrine infusion
CFor profound shock
CDoses of 2 to 10 mg/kg per min stimulate b1 receptors; milder a (vasoconstrictor) effects.
•Increased rate (b) and systemic resistance (a) can increase myocardial workload and ischemia.
•Catecholamines can precipitate coronary vasoconstriction.
•Extravasation can cause tissue ischemia and sloughing.
•Effects profoundly increased in patients on MAO inhibitors or with pheochromocytoma.
•Dopamine = short half-life; with rapid discontinuation, bradycardia and hypotension can return.
•Double the concentration if the initial low-dose infusion (1 to 4 mg/min) is inadequate
•AV block diagnoses
•Reinforce how often patients can be stable without spending time analyzing the exact site and nature of the block.
•Patients requiring repeated atropine, TCP, or catecholamine infusions will require transvenous pacing as quickly as possible.
•Always start making arrangements for transvenous pacing for stable patients with new infra-nodal blocks.
